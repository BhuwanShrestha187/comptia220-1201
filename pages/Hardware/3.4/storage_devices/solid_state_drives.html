SSD Types & Interfaces

SATA SSD

  • Uses standard SATA III interface (6 Gbps max)
  • 2.5-inch form factor (same as laptop HDDs)
  • Backward compatible with older SATA ports
  • Cheaper than NVMe, good for upgrades

NVMe SSD (PCIe)

  • Uses PCIe lanes (much faster: PCIe 3.0 â†’ ~3.5 GB/s, PCIe 4.0 â†’ ~7 GB/s)
  • M.2 form factor (most common) or U.2 / E1.S in enterprise
  • Requires M.2 slot with PCIe support (not SATA-only)
  • Best performance for OS/boot drive

SSD Advantages & Failure Symptoms

Advantages over HDD

  • No moving parts â†’ faster, silent, more shock-resistant
  • Lower latency, higher IOPS
  • Lower power consumption
  • Faster boot & app load times

Common Failure Symptoms

  • System freezes or BSOD during heavy I/O
  • SMART warnings / health dropping rapidly
  • Sudden inability to boot (no OS found)
  • Files become corrupted or inaccessible
  • Very slow performance (firmware bug or wear leveling failure)
  • SSD disappears from BIOS / OS

Monitoring SSD Health

Key Tools:

  • CrystalDiskInfo (Windows) â€“ shows SMART attributes, temperature, TBW used

  • Windows: wmic diskdrive get status or Storage settings
  • Critical SMART attributes to watch: Wear Leveling Count, Reallocated Sectors, Available Spare
